U.S. Pork Exports to China: A Shrinking Market Share

How competitors captured China's pork import market

By Yating Wang

Pork is the backbone of Chinese diets - economical, protein-rich, and deeply cultural. From street-food dumplings to braised belly, it delivers accessible nutrition to hundreds of millions. Despite price fluctuations and modest declines in meat consumption share, as shown in a recent research, pork still accounts for over half of consumer meat expenditures in China.

In the past decade, the U.S. has been one of China's top pork suppliers, exporting both muscle meat and edible offals like pig feet. But that trade relationship is now under threat. After President Trump raised the total tariff to 104% on all Chinese imports, China retaliated with another round of tariff, resulting in a total 84% tariff on imported U.S. goods, according to the Office of the Tariff Commission of the State Council.

In 2016-2018, the U.S. and Canada were China's primary pork suppliers, with the U.S. holding steady at about 22-25% market share.

The U.S. emerged as a top beneficiary during the 2019-2020 African Swine Fever crisis, when China's domestic production plummeted by 40%.

By 2021, competitors like Brazil and Spain began capturing market share as China diversified its pork sources.

Trade tensions and higher tariffs made U.S. pork less competitive, with market share dropping to 7.7% by 2022.

In 2024, Canada has regained dominance with 77% share, while the U.S. accounts for less than 5% of China's pork imports.

The decline of U.S. pork exports to China represents a significant shift in global agricultural trade patterns. As Chinese consumers continue to demand high-quality pork products, American producers face increasing competition from other exporting nations that have capitalized on the U.S.-China trade tensions.

Experts suggest that regaining market share will require not only resolution of trade disputes but also significant investment in marketing and relationship-building with Chinese importers and consumers.
